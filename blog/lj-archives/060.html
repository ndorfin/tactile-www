The beat reaches a crescendo. Whirring undertones compliment the forward-facing attention-grabbing vocals. People on their bikes are singing.

We're moving to the beat. Cylindrical. I feel alive. Your rhythm matches mine, I speed up and so do you within half a beat. You're mocking me. I tire and slow down, you use the opportunity to fake-wipe the sweat from your brow while waiting for me to catch that second wind. Always in time.

The sounds going through my ears propels me, as if the energy stored in a song is a catalyst for my own internal reaction. My legs fire like pistons, plummeting the pedals down and the opposing stroke pulling the pedal up again. Alternating, Current. Current feeds the dynamo that is us. We're finally giving back, giving and not just taking.

The music is not just inside me. It is us. We are singing. Our bodies the instrument.

I thought about the concept of a gym being a source of energy. A generator. Why not replace the useless resistance on an exercise bike with a dynamo. If you want to run a high resistance, the more power you provide.

It reminded me of a concept that hit the mainstream news a while back. A building was being designed that would use the energy given off by people using the stairs. While the energy used in climbing stairs is not enough to run all the services in a building, it will have an impact, heck, even the lightbulbs illuminating the stairwells could run off the users climbing those stairs.

The BBC published an article about using the energy given off by humans in simple and elegant ways, we're not talking Matrix style harvest farms here. Coincidentally, one of the comments raised in the article suggests gym equipment doing the same.

"My first thought is that to encourage this sort of behaviour and investment for the sprung floor electricity generation would be a night club. Michael, Hampshire

[Emphasis my own]

The amount of energy given would directly influence your environment, in wonderfully positive ways.

The question now is: "Is it more expensive in terms of energy to create and implement this technology, than just expending your energy as normal?" How's that for capitalist thinking. :(

Further research

I happened across a related article on the BBC website, which made for more fascinating reading. Sadly, I reckon the amount of energy spent getting to and from the gym would negate any energy you produced for the gym. Isn't our goal then to find more efficient ways of capturing energy?

Isn't a small reduction in energy consumption worth it anyway?

Pass it on!

The quest for happiness is a long and complicated one. You have to be ready to be happy, much the same way you need to be ready to awaken. It's not something you can just switch over to.
Overcoming depression is a whole other journey before the quest for happiness. As Jax would know, in my pursuit of happiness I decided I didn't have to be depressed anymore. Why be depressed, when you can be happy? Depression is addictive in many ways, but so too is happiness.

I was thinking last night (whilst filling up my car) that I should share a tip for being more upbeat.

I think one of the primary ways to be happy is to surround yourself with happy people. While happiness is a deeply internal feeling, it can be influenced by external factors - We're emotional beings afterall (well, most of us :P). Music can shift your mood instantly.
So, back to happy people. I've recently been getting a buzz from the man and woman in the street, the average Joe. I used to be quite uncomfortable around strangers, especially people who come out of the shadows of your awareness and into the spotlight for brief periods. Case in point: Petrol Attendants.
These guys (and in rare cases: ladies) work the worst hours, get paid peanuts and probably have to deal with some seriously grumpy people. Picture it, they walk over from the shop area, expecting the same thing they've come to expect. Very rarely have I come across a petrol attendant who has a genuine smile on his face.
So, instead of waiting for them to pep me up, I try.

I start with greeting them in their native tongue. (Thankfully it is relatively easy in the Cape to recognise a Xhosa person, as it's the most used black language down here.)
"Molo!" I say with a smile. Instinctively, their answer is "Hello". Then a small smile creeps in as they realise this white boy in front of them made the effort to communicate in their home tongue. I ask them how they are. "Khunjani?!". Now at this point, the mini-conversation can go two ways. Either they'll enthusiastically rattle off in Xhosa about anything and everything and I won't understand, Or they'll revert to English or Afrikaans. If the latter, I ask them to teach me a Xhosa word, any Xhosa word. My pronounciation isn't too sharp, but that makes it all the more fun.
If the former, I respond with: "Haibo! Undie-yaaaz" (pronounced that way - don't ask me how it's spelt! :P) which, when translated means: "Gosh! I don't understand." heh. (see: Learn Xhosa: MP3s)

I learnt this trick when in Mexico with Gareth and Mandy. One of the first afternoons there, I'd walk up to the bar and ask in English for another beer. Most of the poor mexicans behind the bar would get the beer with a blank look on their face. Something a robot could do.
Gareth suggested we learn some basic spanish and start using it. Advice that proved priceless.
The next time I needed a topup, I walked up to the bar and said: "Beunos tardes! Uno cerveza por favor". The smile effect was addictive. I drank a lot of Corona and Margerhitas in Mexico :)

To sum it up, don't wait for others to cheer you up. Make them smile and in so doing, you too will feel happy. :)

I'm realising now, that reading all those self-help articles on managing depression make points very similar to those I've outlined already. Amazing how you don't actually see something when you don't look for it. Simple tips like smiling, so difficult when depressed, and yet so easy to do.

Everything you think is wrong

I met her at the train station I was never meant to stop at.

The train screeched to a halt. Handbags and brollies rocketed down from various locations within the cab as I struggled to maintain my hold on the silver isle pole. The smell of freshly welded metal, the smell of averted disaster.
Most of the passengers remained unscathed in the brutal attack of their senses. The train suffered no collision, just a few flustered heart-rates amongst the survivors and a murmur before the crying started.
I stumbled out the emergency doorway and took the service-walkway to the station landing just ahead. There, onlookers gasped and recounted the story to those not privileged enough to see the event with their own eyes.

A woman in her late twenties stepped forward from the group.
"Are you alright!?!" She gaped.
"I think so" I said. "Can you help me assist the other passengers?"
"Why... um, yeah, sure!" she exclaimed.

I slowly turned for the train in the tunnel.

An explosion of senses erupted from her hand as she grabbed my shoulder. I was both bouncing with joy and crying morbid tears typically left for the passing of a loved-family member. I was flying at will and dragging myself through the mud. Flashes of light and memories before my eyes.
Everything faded to black as I collapsed in a heap, my brain overloaded from the total onslaught of senses.

I was sleeping.

When I came to, I was being lifted by two medics onto a stretcher. Apparently I had passed out next to the rails. I tried to sit up in the stretcher, look past my legs, between the two medics at my feet and there it was, the tunnel. Empty. No train. No people. My head hurt.

A Slideshow of Gulls

I was browsing a set of photos and came across something I thought I had to share with my younger brother Mike.

It was series of clips taken out on the ocean on a foggy day. There in the still water was a craggy rock, just exposed enough for a gang of gulls to perch upon it. In the series of pics, the water was visibly surging to the right of this gull encrusted rock until the last pic, which was filled with a total white-wash of water. Seemingly obliterating the gulls too, but it felt as if they were OK and managed to take off in time.

I called Mike over to show him the hysterical nature of the photos and started the slideshow. Mike and I were instantaneously transported to the water, where we waded long enough to see the slideshow before our own eyes. It was even more spectacular in person. Catching our breaths from all the laughter, we decided to turn and make for the coast (which, based on the direction of the wave was now in front of us.) We couldn't see anything through the thick fog, a fog which seemed to blend the colour of the ocean with that of the sky above.

Mike wasn't swimming very well in the warm water, so he clung on to my left foot as I swam with the waves towards the coast (which had to be there). Slowly the rocky coastline emerged from the fog and we were well within reach. I had a distinct impression there were sharks in the water, so I looked down into the water and lo and behold, circling Great Whites! They were juvenile sharks, not more than 2 meters long. But they were there, and circling still.

I encouraged Mike to remain calm and just to carry on paddling, we were almost there. The sharks knew this too and just as I reached for the nearest rock on the coast, I felt Mike's grip on my left ankle subside. I woke up.
